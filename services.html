Individual Psychotherapy

Generally speaking, individual psychotherapy tends to focus on your childhood, past experiences and current concerns or problems. Through the process of individual therapy, one can often gain an understanding of why they feel and behave the way they do. Issues, concerns and problems brought to therapy are often successfully resolved. Additionally, individual psychotherapy yields itself to: personal growth, self-actualization, acceptance, and increased self-esteem, alleviation of guilt, pain, and undue hardship.

Individuals often come to therapy to work on a variety of issues. Issues range from anxiety, depression, low self-esteem, grief & loss, phobias, compulsions, quest for meaning, relationship concerns, and—increasingly more common—unresolved feelings of guilt surrounding wealth and success.

Within the 50-minute hour, I invite you to share your beliefs, feelings, thoughts, and concerns. Throughout our work together, we will utilize this information in an effort to help you understand how your (possibly negative) thought patterns might influence your behavior. Within this safe, confidential, and non-judgmental space, I hope to encourage you to confront and take charge of your issues, concerns, and problems, and to grow as an individual.

I encourage you to learn more about who you are, so you may enjoy life more fully. By working to dissipate your unique obstacles, roadblocks and barriers, we will discover ways to allow you to pursue the life you want to lead. I invite you to find joy, peace, empathy and an increased sense of self in therapy.

Couples Therapy

Couples therapy is a lot like individual psychotherapy, except with two people. This, of course, changes the nature of the sessions from "I" to "we." In individual therapy, the session is all about you. In a couples therapy session, an undercurrent of teamwork and collaboration is a must. Within a couples therapy dynamic, the couple's therapist is not neutral. I work to help you improve your communication techniques with each other in an effort for you to possess the highest chance of success.

As with individual therapy, couples therapy builds on stated goals, objectives, desires, and a willingness to do the work—in this case, together. You learn about yourself, your partner, and create a dialogue where you learn what works and what does not. A lot of people aim to learn what kind of life they want with a partner, who they want to be as a partner, and what they might like their partner to bring to the relationship. Although the therapy takes place witht he couple in mind, an individual's willingness to be open to changing one's self is extremely beneficial. Having a vision, positive attitude, the motivation to participate, and the willingness to learn and adapt are all positive components for success.

In couples therapy, the importance of effective communication cannot be stressed enough. Each partner must be willing to respect the other partner, be open to his or her partner's views and opinions, and be persistent about working together. Communicating effectively can be a challenge. Taking the time to be empathic towards one another, allowing the time needed to understand and be understood, and being patient with one another is key. Through this shared communication, the couple can grow together, develop a greater understanding of one another, better pursue shared goals, and improve upon many other aspects of their relationship according to their state desires.

Group Therapy

Group therapy serves as a complementary addition to individual psychotherapy or couples therapy, but can easily stand alone as a single therapeutic intervention as well. In most cases, group psychotherapy consists of a small number of people (usually 4 to 8 participants) and one or two trained psychotherapists who serve as facilitators to guide and lead the group.

The type of group varies greatly, not only in subject matter but in structure and timing. While some groups are highly structured, others are more open and varied. While some groups have pre-determined start and finish dates, others are on-going for an undetermined amount of time. Regardless if the type of groups that I offer, commitment and adherence to group norms are essential. Within this dynamic, group therapy offers participants the unique opportunity to openly share issues, concerns, or problems with other members, and to give and receive guidance, support, and understanding in return. Because this platform provides space for participants to "test the waters" in a safe, controlled, confidential setting, one direct result of participation is often the improvement of interpersonal relationship skills. A translation of these skills to their own life allows participants to improve communication with, better understand, and relate to others in their academic, work, and social settings.
