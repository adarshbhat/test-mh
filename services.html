Individual Psychotherapy

Generally speaking, individual psychotherapy tends to focus on your childhood, past experiences and current concerns or problems. Through the process of individual therapy, one can often gain an understanding of why they feel and behave the way they do. Issues, concerns and problems brought to therapy are often successfully resolved. Additionally, individual psychotherapy yields itself to: personal growth, self-actualization, acceptance, increased self-esteem, alleviation of guilt, pain, and undue hardship.

Individuals often come to therapy to work on a variety of issues. Issues range from anxiety, depression, low self-esteem, grief & loss, phobias, compulsions, existential angst, relationship concerns, and increasingly more common: unresolved feelings of guilt surrounding wealth and success.

Within the 50-minute hour, you are invited to share your beliefs, feelings, thoughts, and concerns. Throughout our work together, this information will be utilized in an effort to help you understand how your (sometimes negative) thought patterns might influence your behavior. Within this safe, confidential, and non-judgmental space, you are encouraged to confront and take charge of your issues, concerns, and problems.

I encourage you to learn more about who you are, so you may enjoy life more fully. By working through your unique obstacles, roadblocks and barriers, interferences preventing you from having the life you want often dissipate. I invite you to find joy, peace, empathy and an increased sense of self in therapy.

Couples Therapy

Couples therapy is a lot like individual psychotherapy, except with two people — this of course changes the course of what a session looks like. In individual therapy, the session is all about you. In a couples therapy session, an undercurrent of teamwork and collaboration is a must. Within a couples therapy dynamic, the couples therapist is not neutral. I work to help you improve your communication techniques with each other in an effort for you to possess the highest chance of success.

Like with individual therapy, couples therapy builds on stated goals, objectives, desires, and a willingness to do the work — in this case, together. You learn about yourself, your partner, and create a dialogue where you learn what works and what does not. A lot of people aim to learn what kind of life they want with a partner, who they want to be as a partner, and what they might like their partner to bring to the relationship. Having a vision, positive attitude, the motivation to participate and willingness to learn and adapt (being open to changing one's self is extremely beneficial) are all positive components for success.

In couples therapy, the importance of effective community cannot be stressed enough. Each partner must be willing to respect the other partner, be open to his or her partner's views and opinions, and be persistent. Communicating effectively can be a challenge. Taking the time to be empathic towards one another, allowing the time needed to understand and be understood, and being patient with one another is key.

Group Therapy

Group therapy serves as a complementary addition to individual psychotherapy or couples therapy, but can stand alone as a single therapeutic intervention as well. In most cases, group psychotherapy consists of a small number of people (usually between 4-8) and one or two trained psychotherapists serving also as facilitators to guide and lead the group.

Within this dynamic, participants are given the unique opportunity to openly share issues, concerns or problems with other members, and receive/give guidance, support, and understanding. Because this platform provides space for participants to "test the waters" in a safe, controlled, confidential setting, and improvement in interpersonal relationships are often a direct result of participation. These skills gained often translate to a better understanding of how they relate to others in academic, work, and other social settings.

Groups vary greatly. Some groups are highly structured, while others are more open variation. While some groups have pre-determined start and finished dates, others are on-going for an undetermined amount of time. Regardless of the type of group being offered, commitment and adherence to group norms are essential.
